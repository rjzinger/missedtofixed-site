# A2P 10DLC Consent Description — paste this into Twilio

This is the text to paste into the **"How do end-users consent to receive messages? (40-2048 characters)"** field on the Twilio A2P campaign form when you go back to finish registration.

This is the field carriers scrutinize most. The wording below is structured to (a) explicitly state the call-as-consent model, which is the legally recognized opt-in for missed-call-recovery services, (b) reference the privacy policy, and (c) describe the opt-out mechanism — all of which the reviewer is looking for.

---

## Paste exactly this:

```
End users opt in by placing a phone call to a business that uses the Missed to Fixed service. When the call is not answered within the business's configured ring time, the call is automatically forwarded to a Twilio phone number owned by the business. The act of placing this call constitutes consent to receive a single automated SMS reply from the business and to exchange a short series of text messages capturing the reason for the call so the business can return it. This is the standard call-as-consent opt-in model for missed-call recovery services.

Each business using the service publishes its phone number on its own marketing channels (website, Google Business Profile, signage, vehicles, etc.), so consumers who call the number have done so deliberately to reach that business.

End users may withdraw consent at any time by replying STOP, UNSUBSCRIBE, QUIT, CANCEL, or END to any message; no further messages will be sent. Replying HELP returns help instructions and contact info. Opt-in details, privacy practices, and message frequency are documented at https://missedtofixed.com/privacy and https://missedtofixed.com/terms.

No marketing or promotional messages are sent through this campaign. All messages are transactional follow-ups initiated by the consumer's own call.
```

---

## Why this wording works

- **First paragraph** establishes the consent mechanism (the call) in unambiguous language carriers recognize.
- **Second paragraph** establishes that the consumer knew they were calling a business, not a random number.
- **Third paragraph** covers opt-out (STOP and four synonyms) and HELP, references both legal URLs.
- **Fourth paragraph** says explicitly that this isn't a marketing campaign — important because Sole Proprietor campaigns get extra scrutiny on this point.

If Twilio's reviewer comes back with questions, the most likely is "give an example of how a consumer arrives at your number." You'd answer with: a homeowner finds a plumber on Google Business Profile, calls the listed number, doesn't reach anyone, and ~20 seconds later gets a text from that plumber's number saying "sorry we missed you."
